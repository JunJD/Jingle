# Openwork/Jingle: A Desktop Workspace For Delegated Agent Work

[中文](./product-launch-introduction-cn.md)

AI agents are becoming capable enough to touch real work. That is exactly why
they need a better product surface than a blank prompt box.

The hard question is no longer only whether a model can call a tool. The hard
question is whether a person can understand what the agent is doing, decide what
it is allowed to do next, and recover when something goes wrong.

That is the product shape Openwork/Jingle is exploring: a desktop workspace for
delegated agent work.

The first move should be fast. You should be able to open a launcher, search,
start a task, and get out of the way. But the launcher is only the beginning.
Once work starts, the product has to own the lifetime of that work.

A delegated task should have a visible place to happen. It should show what
context is being used, what actions are being attempted, what needs approval,
what changed, and what evidence was left behind. It should be possible to pause,
return, continue, or branch without losing the thread.

This is the difference between making an agent feel magical and making it feel
trustworthy.

Magic hides the machinery. Trust makes the right parts visible. When software
can read files, run commands, call services, and change things on your behalf,
control cannot be a sentence in the marketing copy. It has to appear at the
moment of risk.

The product stance is local-first, but not isolationist. Models and connected
services may still live outside the machine. The important boundary is that the
work state, the history, the approvals, the diagnostics, and the user's sense of
control begin on the user's computer.

That is the experience we want to make legible:

start quickly, delegate clearly, inspect the work, approve the risky parts, and
come back later without losing the trail.

An agent workspace is not just a chat box with more tools. It is a place where
work has a lifecycle, and where the person remains present in that lifecycle
without having to micromanage every step.
